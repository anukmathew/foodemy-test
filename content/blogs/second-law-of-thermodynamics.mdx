# ENTROPY CHANGES IN A CLOSED SYSTEM

Entropy change in a closed system provides a quantitative measure of the degree of irreversibility associated with a process. In thermodynamics, entropy is regarded as a property that indicates the direction of energy transfer and the degradation of energy quality. For a closed system, the entropy change is determined by the balance between entropy transfer across the system boundary, typically through heat interactions, and entropy generation within the system due to irreversibilities such as friction, unrestrained expansion, mixing, or heat transfer through a finite temperature difference. Thus, analyzing entropy changes in a closed system is fundamental to assessing process efficiency and compliance with the second law of thermodynamics. In this section, we will begin with the entropy change of an **ideal gas in the general case** and then analyse its behaviour for specific thermodynamic processes such as **isothermal, adiabatic, isobaric, and isochoric transformations**.

## General Case

Suppose a unit mass of gas at pressure $p_1$, volume $v_1$ and temperature $T_1$ having entropy $s_1$ be heated so that the final temperature, pressure, volume and entropy are $T_2$, $p_2$, $v_2$ and $s_2$ respectively.

Suppose $dQ$ be the small change in heat, $du$ be the small change in internal energy and $dW$ be the small amount of work done. Then, according to the law of conservation of energy,

$$
dQ=du+dW
$$

Now, we know

$$
du=c_vdT
$$

And

$$
dW=pdv
$$

So, we can write

$$
dQ=\ c_vdT+pdv
$$

Dividing the expression by T, we get

$$
\frac{dQ}{T}=\frac{c_vdT}{T}+\frac{pdv}{T}
$$

Now, we know

$$
ds=\frac{dQ}{T}
$$

And from

$$
pv=RT\Longrightarrow\frac{p}{T}=\frac{R}{v}
$$

We get

$$
ds=\frac{c_vdT}{T}+\frac{Rdv}{v}
$$

Upon integration, we get

$$
\int_{s_1}^{s_2}ds=\int_{T_1}^{T_2}{\frac{c_v}{T}dT}+\int_{v_1}^{v_2}{R\frac{dv}{v}}\Longrightarrow\left(s_2-s_1\right)=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}+R\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}\ldots\ldots(1)
$$

Now, from ideal gas equation, we can write

$$
\frac{p_1v_1}{T_1}=\frac{p_2v_2}{T_2}\Longrightarrow\frac{T_2}{T_1}=\frac{p_2v_2}{p_1v_1}\Longrightarrow\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}=\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}+\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}
$$

Substituting in (1), we get

$$
\left(s_2-s_1\right)=c_v\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}+c_v\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}+R\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}=c_v\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}+\left(c_v+R\right)\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}\ldots\ldots (2)
$$

We know that for ideal gas,

$$
c_p-c_v=R\Longrightarrow c_p=c_v+R
$$

So, from (2), we get

$$
\left(s_2-s_1\right)=c_v\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}+c_p\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}\ldots\ldots(3)
$$

Again, from ideal gas equation, we can write

$$
\frac{p_1v_1}{T_1}=\frac{p_2v_2}{T_2}\Longrightarrow\frac{v_2}{v_1}=\frac{p_1T_2}{p_2T_1}\Longrightarrow\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}=\ln{\frac{p_1}{p_2}}+\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}
$$

Substituting in (1), we get

$$
\left(s_2-s_1\right)=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}+R\ln{\frac{p_1}{p_2}}+R\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}=\left(c_v+R\right)\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}+R\ln{\frac{p_1}{p_2}}
$$

Substituting $c_p=c_v+R$, we get

$$
\left(s_2-s_1\right)=c_p\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}-R\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}\ldots.(4)
$$

Therefore, (2), (3) and (4) gives the expression for entropy change per unit mass of gas in the general case.

Now we shall consider the change in entropy in specific thermodynamical processes.

## Isochoric

Suppose unit mass of gas is heated under constant volume. Let the temperature rise from $T_1$ to $T_2$ and the corresponding entropy change be from $s_1$ to $s_2$. Let the amount of heat be Q. Then

$$
Q=c_v(T_2-T_1)
$$

In differential terms,

$$
dQ=c_vdT
$$

Dividing by T, we get

$$
\frac{dQ}{T}=c_v\frac{dT}{T}
$$

We know

$$
ds=\frac{dQ}{T}
$$

So, we have

$$
ds=c_v\frac{dT}{T}
$$

Integrating, we have

$$
\int_{s_1}^{s_2}ds=\int_{T_1}^{T_2}c_v\frac{dT}{T}\Longrightarrow s_2-s_1=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}
$$

The above expression can be derived directly using (1), by putting $$v_1=v_2$$

## Isobaric

Suppose unit mass of gas is heated under constant pressure. Let the temperature rise from $T_1$ to $T_2$ and the corresponding entropy change be from $s_1$ to $s_2$. Let the amount of heat be Q. Then

$$
Q=c_p(T_2-T_1)
$$

In differential terms,

$$
dQ=c_pdT
$$

Dividing by T, we get

$$
\frac{dQ}{T}=c_p\frac{dT}{T}
$$

We know

$$
ds=\frac{dQ}{T}
$$

So, we have

$$
ds=c_p\frac{dT}{T}
$$

Integrating, we have

$$
\int_{s_1}^{s_2}ds=\int_{T_1}^{T_2}c_p\frac{dT}{T}\Longrightarrow s_2-s_1=c_p\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}
$$

The above expression can be derived directly using (3), by putting $p_1=p_2$

## Isothermal

During isothermal process, we have $Q=W$. Therefore, the entropy change from $s_1$ to $s_2$ by absorbing heat equals the work done by the gas during expansion.

$$
Q=\int_{s_1}^{s_2}dT=T(s_2-s_1)
$$

Also

$$
W=p_1v_1\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}=RT_1\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}
$$

Since, the process is isothermal, so temperature is constant. Therefore, $T=T_1$. Thus, we have

$$
T\left(s_2-s_1\right)=RT\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}\Longrightarrow\left(s_2-s_1\right)=R\ln{\frac{v_2}{v_1}}
$$

The above expression can also be obtained alternatively using (1) by substituting $T_2=T_1$

## Adiabatic

As heat is neither supplied nor rejected during an adiabatic process, we have

$$
dQ=0\Longrightarrow\frac{dQ}{dT}=0\Longrightarrow ds=0\Longrightarrow s=constant
$$

Thus, during an adiabatic process there is no change in entropy. Hence, it is also known as isentropic process.

## Polytropic process

For a polytropic process, we have

$$
pv^n=constant
$$

Therefore, we have

$$
p_1v_1^n=p_2v_2^n\Longrightarrow\frac{p_1}{p_2}=\left(\frac{v_2}{v_1}\right)^n
$$

Also, from ideal gas equation, we have

$$
\frac{p_1v_1}{T_1}=\frac{p_2v_2}{T_2}\Longrightarrow\frac{p_1}{p_2}=\frac{v_2}{v_1}\times\frac{T_1}{T_2}
$$

Combining, we get

$$
\frac{v_2}{v_1}\times\frac{T_1}{T_2}=\left(\frac{v_2}{v_1}\right)^n\Longrightarrow\frac{T_1}{T_2}=\left(\frac{v_2}{v_1}\right)^{n-1}\Longrightarrow\frac{v_2}{v_1}=\left(\frac{T_1}{T_2}\right)^\frac{1}{n-1}
$$

Substituting in (1), we get

$$
\begin{align*}
\left(s_2-s_1\right)&=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}+R\ln{\left(\frac{T_1}{T_2}\right)^\frac{1}{n-1}} \\

&=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}-R\frac{1}{n-1}\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}} \\

&=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}-\left(c_p-c_v\right)\frac{1}{n-1}\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}} \\

&=c_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}-\left(\gamma c_v-c_v\right)\frac{1}{n-1}\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}} \\

&=c_v\left[1-\frac{\gamma-1}{n-1}\right]\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}} \\

&=c_v\left[\frac{n-1-\gamma+1}{n-1}\right]\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}\\

&=c_v\left[\frac{n-\gamma}{n-1}\right]\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}\\
\end{align*}
$$

# ENTROPY CHANGES IN AN OPEN SYSTEM

We start with the **second law of thermodynamics for a closed system**:

$$
dS_{sys}\geq\frac{\delta Q}{T_b}
$$

Where ${dS}_{sys}$ is the change in entropy of the system, and $\frac{\delta Q}{T_b}$ represents entropy transfer due to heat across the boundary at boundary temperature $T_b$.
In an **open system**, mass flows in and out of the control volume, and since mass carries entropy, we must include this transport.
So, the **rate form of entropy balance** becomes:

$$
\frac{dS_{CV}}{dt}=Entropy\ transfer\ with\ heat+Entropy\ transfer\ with\ mass+Entropy\ generation
$$

If heat transfer occurs across several parts of the boundary, each at temperature $T_j$, the entropy transfer rate is:

$$
{\dot{S}}_Q=\sum\frac{{\dot{Q}}_j}{T_j}
$$

Each mass flow entering or leaving carries entropy s with it. Entropy carried into the control volume

$$
{\dot{S}}_{mass,\ in}=\sum{{\dot{m}}_{in}s_{in}}
$$

Entropy carried out of the control volume

$$
{\dot{S}}_{mass,\ out}=\sum{{\dot{m}}_{out}s_{out}}
$$

Due to **irreversibilities** inside the control volume (friction, mixing, chemical reactions, finite temperature heat transfer, etc.), entropy is generated. Let this be:

$$
{\dot{S}}_{gen}\geq0
$$

Combining all terms, the **general entropy balance for an open system** is:

$$
\frac{dS_{CV}}{dt}=\sum\frac{{\dot{Q}}_j}{T_j}+\sum{{\dot{m}}_{in}s_{in}}-\sum{{\dot{m}}_{out}s_{out}}+{\dot{S}}_{gen}
$$

For steady-state devices (turbines, compressors, nozzles, etc.), the entropy stored in the control volume does not change with time:

$$
\frac{dS_{CV}}{dt}=0
$$

Therefore,

$$
\sum\frac{{\dot{Q}}_j}{T_j}+\sum{{\dot{m}}_{in}s_{in}}=\sum{{\dot{m}}_{out}s_{out}}-{\dot{S}}_{gen}
$$

This is the **entropy balance equation for steady-flow systems**.

# AVAILABILITY

In thermodynamics, **availability** (also called _exergy_) represents the **maximum useful work** that can be extracted from a system as it comes to equilibrium with a specified environment (the “dead state”).

Unlike energy, which is always conserved, **availability is destroyed** when irreversibilities (friction, mixing, unrestrained expansion, heat transfer through a finite temperature difference, etc.) occur. Thus, availability is a measure of the _quality_ of energy.

- Energy: Quantity is conserved.
- Exergy: Quality is degraded in real processes.

Suppose that a Carnot engine is operating between the reservoir at temperature $T$ and the surrounding with temperature $T_0$. The amount of work obtained is

$$
W=Q\eta=Q\left(1-\frac{T_0}{T}\right)=Q-T_0\frac{Q}{T}=Q-T_0|\Delta S|
$$

Where the change in the entropy of the reservoir is shown by $\Delta S$.

The portion of energy which is not available for conversion to work is called unavailable energy or Anergy.

## Loss of available energy

Consider a reversible engine. Let $Q$ amount of heat be absorbed by the engine. Suppose initial temperature be $T_1$ and the final temperature be $T_2$. Let the temperature of the surroundings be $T_0$

- Initial available energy $=Q\left(1-\frac{T_0}{T_1}\right)$
- Final available energy $=Q\left(1-\frac{T_0}{T_2}\right)$

Therefore, the loss of available energy is

$$
=Q\left[\left(1-\frac{T_0}{T_1}\right)-\left(1-\frac{T_0}{T_2}\right)\right]=T_0\left[\frac{Q}{T_2}-\frac{Q}{T_1}\right]=T_0\Delta S_{universe}
$$

Where $\Delta S_{universe}$ is the change in the entropy of the Universe. The second law says that $\Delta S_{universe} \geq 0$ (equality holds for reversible processes). Hence, for all irreversible processes occurs with the loss of energy.

## Availability: Non-flow process

Consider the pressure and temperature of the surrounding to be $P_0$ and $T_0$. Let the initial and final volume of the system be $V_1$ and $V_0$. The final pressure of the system is same as that of the surrounding as it comes into equilibrium with the surroundings. Let $\Delta V$ denote the change in volume of the system. Then

$$
\Delta V=V_0-V_1
$$

However, the pressure in the surrounding resists this change in volume. Therefore, the work done is

$$
P_0(V_0-V_1)
$$

Therefore, the available work is the maximum work obtained from the system minus the above work. Thus, availability is given by

$$
Availability=\ W_{useful}=W_{max}-P_0(V_0-V_1)
$$

Let the temperature of the surrounding be $T_0$. We know

$$
dQ=dU+dW\Longrightarrow dW=dQ-dU\Longrightarrow dW=TdS-dU
$$

Thus,

$$
W_{max}=T_0\left(S_0-S_1\right)-\left(U_0-U_1\right)=\left(U_1-T_0S_1\right)-(U_0-T_0S_0)
$$

Thus,

$$
\begin{align*}
Availability&=\left(U_1-T_0S_1\right)-\left(U_0-T_0S_0\right)-P_0\left(V_0-V_1\right)\\

&=\left(U_1+P_0V_1-T_0S_1\right)-\left(U_0+P_0V_0-T_0S_0\right)
\end{align*}
$$

We can write

$$
Availability=\ \varphi_1-\varphi_0
$$

where

$$
\varphi=U+P_0V-T_0S
$$

is called the availability function.

Suppose a system goes from state 1 to state 2, then change in availability is given by

$$Change\ in\ availability=\ \varphi_1-\varphi_2$$

## Availability : Steady flow Process

The SFEE can be written as

$$
\dot{Q}-{\dot{W}}_{shaft}=\dot{m}\left(h_o+\frac{1}{2}V_o^2+gz_o\right)-\dot{m}\left(h_i+\frac{1}{2}V_i^2+gz_i\right)
$$

From second law, we have

$$
\dot{Q}=\dot{m}T_0(s_o-s_i)
$$

Therefore,

$$
{\dot{W}}_{shaft}=\dot{m}\left(h_i+\frac{1}{2}V_i^2+gz_i\right)-\dot{m}\left(h_o+\frac{1}{2}V_o^2+gz_o\right)-\dot{m}T_0(s_i-s_o)
$$

Or

$$
{\dot{W}}_{shaft}=\dot{m}\left(h_i+\frac{1}{2}V_i^2+gz_i-T_0s_i\right)-\dot{m}\left(h_o+\frac{1}{2}V_o^2+gz_o-T_0s_0\right)
$$

Or

$$
{\dot{W}}_{shaft}=\dot{m}\left(h_i+{KE}_i+{PE}_i-T_0s_i\right)-\dot{m}\left(h_o+{KE}_o+{PE}_o-T_0s_o\right)
$$

The stream availability is given by

$$
\psi=\psi_1-\psi_0
$$

where

$$
\psi=\dot{m}(h+KE+PE-T_0s)
$$

Suppose a system goes from state 1 to state 2, then change in availability is given by

$$
Change\ in\ availability=\ \psi_1-\psi_2
$$

# IRREVERSIBILITY

The difference between the useful work and the actual work is known as the irreversibility of a process. Mathematically,

$$
\begin{align*}
I&=W_{useful}-W \\

&=\left(\varphi_1-\varphi_0\right)-W \\

&=\left(U_1+P_0V_1-T_0S_1\right)-\left(U_0+P_0V_0-T_0S_0\right)-W \\

&=\left(U_1-U_0\right)+T_0\left(S_0-S_1\right)-P_0\left(V_0-V_1\right)-W \\

&=-\Delta U+T_0\Delta S-P_0\Delta V-W
\end{align*}
$$

Now let us assume that the system and the surroundings as a composite system. The work done by this composite system under the adiabatic condition

$$
W=-\Delta U_{sys}+surr=-\Delta U_{sys}-\Delta U_{surr}= -\Delta U-\Delta U_{surr}
$$

$$
\Delta Usurr= -\Delta U-W
$$

Applying first and second law to the surrounding, we get

$$
\Delta U_{surr}=T_0\Delta S_{surr}-P_0\Delta V_{surr}
$$

Therefore, we have

$$
\Delta U_{surr}= -\Delta U-W=T_0\Delta S_{surr}-P_0\Delta V_{surr}
$$

Substituting in the irreversibility expression, we have

$$
I=T_0\Delta S_{surr}-P_0\Delta V_{surr}+T_0\Delta S-P_0\Delta V
$$

$$
=T_0\Delta S+\Delta S_{surr}-P_0(\Delta V+\Delta V_{surr})
$$

As the net volume change of system + surroundings is zero, so we have

$$
I=T_0\Delta S+\Delta S_{surr}=T_0\Delta S_{univ}
$$

For irreversible process,$\Delta S_{univ}>0$ and for reversible process $\Delta S_{univ}=0$

# SECOND LAW EFFICIENCY

The Second-Law Efficiency (also called _exergetic efficiency_) measures how close a real device or process performs compared to the ideal reversible process operating between the same states.

$$
\eta_{II}=\frac{Desired\ Output}{Maximum\ Possible\ (Reversible)\ Output}
$$

It is also expressed as

$$
\eta_{II}=\frac{Exergy\ Recovered\ (or\ Utilised)}{Exergy\ Supplied}
$$

## Different Forms

### For a heat engine

$$
\eta_{II}=\frac{\eta_{actual}}{\eta_{carnot}}
$$

### For refrigerator/heat pump

$$
\eta_{II}=\frac{{COP}_{actual}}{{COP}_{carnot}}
$$

### For general processes

$$
\eta_{II}=1-\frac{Irreversibility}{Exergy\ Supplied}=\frac{{\dot{W}}_{rev}}{{\dot{W}}_{rev}+I}
$$

# THIRD LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS

In our earlier discussion on **irreversibility**, we emphasized that all real processes involve generation of entropy. The second law of thermodynamics makes this clear: in a reversible process, entropy remains conserved, whereas in an irreversible process, entropy of the universe always increases. This principle sets a **direction to natural processes**—heat flows spontaneously from high to low temperature, work cannot be completely converted into heat and back, and frictional effects always degrade useful work potential.

Now, if irreversibility and entropy generation are unavoidable, a natural question arises: _what happens to entropy as we approach the extreme lower limit of temperature, absolute zero?_ If entropy keeps increasing in real processes, is there a universal lower bound to it? Can a system’s entropy ever become negative?

This question leads us to the **Third Law of Thermodynamics**. While the second law governs the **direction and irreversibility** of processes, the third law provides an **absolute reference point for entropy**.

## Statement (Nernst’s Theorem):

_The entropy of a pure, perfectly crystalline substance approaches zero as its temperature approaches absolute zero (0 K)_.

$$
\lim_{ T\to 0}S=0
$$

- At **0 K**, a perfect crystal has complete order; there is only **one possible microstate**.
- Since entropy is given by Boltzmann’s relation,
  $$
  S=k_B\ln{\Omega}
  $$
  and here $\Omega=1\Longrightarrow S=0$

Thus, the Third Law provides an **absolute reference point for entropy**, something the Second Law does not offer.

## Implications of the Third Law

1. **Absolute Entropy Values**
   - Unlike enthalpy or internal energy, entropy can now be assigned an absolute value, not just changes.
   - Entropy values in property tables (steam tables, ideal gas tables) are based on this reference.
2. **Unattainability of Absolute Zero**
   - It is **impossible to reach 0 K** by any finite number of processes. Cooling becomes progressively more difficult as temperature decreases.
   - This principle is crucial in **cryogenics** and sets the ultimate temperature limit in physics.
3. **Heat Capacities Near 0 K**
   - The heat capacities of substances approach zero as temperature approaches absolute zero. This means removing the last bit of energy from a system is practically impossible.
4. **Residual Entropy**
   - Imperfect crystals or disordered solids (e.g., glasses, amorphous solids) may have **non-zero entropy at 0 K**, called **residual entropy**. This reflects molecular disorder even at absolute zero.

## Engineering Relevance

- **Thermodynamic Property Calculations**: Absolute entropies are essential in calculating Gibbs free energy, chemical equilibrium, and phase changes.
- **Cryogenics & Low-Temperature Physics**: The third law sets the limit of refrigeration technologies; liquid helium cooling, superconductivity, and quantum effects are understood in this framework.
- **Irreversibility Link**: While the Second Law explains why entropy always increases in real processes, the Third Law defines the absolute minimum limit of entropy. Together, they give a complete description of entropy behaviour.

# Examples

1.  An ideal gas is heated from temperature $T_1$ to $T_2$ by keeping its volume constant. The gas is expanded back to its initial temperature according to the law ${pv}^n=constant$. If the entropy change in the two processes are equal, then the value of $n$ in terms of the adiabatic index $\gamma$ is
    - (A) $\frac{\gamma+1}{2}$
    - (B) $\frac{\gamma-1}{2}$
    - (C) $\frac{\gamma}{2}$
    - (D) $\gamma-1$

> [!info] Answer
> Change in entropy during a constant volume process is given by
>
> $$
> \Delta S_{isochoric}=mc_v \ln \frac{T_2}{T_1} \ldots \ldots (1)
> $$
>
> Change in entropy during a polytropic process is
>
> $$
> \Delta S\_{polytropic}=mc_v \frac{\gamma - n}{n-1}\ln \frac{T_2}{T_1}\ldots \ldots(2)
> $$
>
> Since the entropy change in both the process is same, so we have
>
> $$
> \begin{align*}
> &\Delta S*{isochoric}=\Delta S*{polytropic}\\
> &\Longrightarrow mc_v\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}=mc_v\left(\frac{\gamma-n}{n-1}\right)\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}\\
> &\Longrightarrow\frac{\gamma-n}{n-1}=1\\
> &\Longrightarrow\gamma-n=n-1\\
> &\Longrightarrow2n=\gamma+1\\
> &\Longrightarrow n=\frac{\gamma+1}{2}
> \end{align*}
> $$
>
> Hence, option (A) is correct.

2.  0.04 kg of carbon dioxide (molecular weight = 44) is compressed from 1 bar, 20 °C, until the pressure is 9 bar, and the volume is then 0.003 m<sup>3</sup>. The change of entropy is ………………. kJ/kgK. Take $c_p$ for carbon dioxide as 0.88 kJ/kg K, and assume carbon dioxide to be a perfect gas.

        :::answer
        Given,

$$

        m=0.04\ kg,\ M=44,\ p_1=1\ bar\ ,\ T_1=293\ K,\ p_2=9\ bar,\ V_2=0.003\ m^3
$$

        Characteristics gas constant is given by

        $$
        R=\frac{8314}{44}=189\ Nm/kgK
        $$

        Final temperature $T_2$ is

        $$
        T_2=\frac{p_2V_2}{mR}=\frac{9\times{10}^5\times0.003}{0.04\times189}=357\ K
        $$

        Change in entropy is

        $$
        \begin{align*}
        \left(s_2-s_1\right)&=c_p\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}-R\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}\\
        &=0.88\ln{\frac{357}{298}}-0.189\ln{\frac{9}{1}}\\
        &=0.1738-0.4153\\
        &=-0.2415\ kJ/kgK\\
        \end{align*}
        $$

    (Negative sign denotes decrease in entropy)
    :::

3.  The change of entropy of 1 kg of air expanding polytropically in a cylinder behind a piston from 7 bar and 600 °C to 1.05 bar is ……………… kJ/kgK. The index of expansion is 1.25.

    :::answer
    Given,

    $$
    p_1=7\ bar,\ T_1=873\ K,\ p_2=1.05\ bar,\ n=1.25,\ m=1kg
    $$

         The final temperature (for a polytropic process) can be found out as
         $$
         \frac{T_2}{T_1}=\left(\frac{p_2}{p_1}\right)^\frac{n-1}{n}\Longrightarrow\frac{T_2}{873}=\left(\frac{1.05}{7}\right)^\frac{1.25-1}{1.25}\Longrightarrow T_2=873\times{0.15}^{0.2}=597.3\ K
         $$
         Now,
         $$
         \gamma=\frac{c_p}{c_v}=\frac{1.005}{0.718}=1.399
         $$
         The change in entropy is
          $$
            \begin{align*}
            &=c_v\left[\frac{n-\gamma}{n-1}\right]\ln{\frac{T_2}{T_1}}\\
            &=0.718\frac{1.25-1.399}{1.25-1}\ln{\frac{597.3}{873}}\\
            &=0.718\times\left(-0.596\right)\times\left(-0.3795\right)\\
            &=0.1626\ kJ/kgK
            \end{align*}
            $$

5.  An ideal gas is compressed reversibly and adiabatically from state $a$ to state $b$. It is then heated reversibly at constant volume to state $c$. After expanding reversibly and adiabatically to state $d$ such that $T_b = T_d$, the gas is again reversibly heated at constant pressure to state $e$ such that $T_e = T_c$. Heat is then rejected reversibly from the gas at constant volume till it returns to state $a$. Then Ta in terms of $T_b$ and $T_c$ is
    - (A) $T_a=\frac{T_c^{\gamma+1}}{T_b^\gamma}$
    - (B) $T_a=\frac{T_b^{\gamma+1}}{T_c^\gamma}$
    - (C) $T_a=\frac{T_b^\gamma}{T_c^{\gamma+1}}$
    - (D) $T_a=\frac{T_b^{\gamma-1}}{T_c^\gamma}$

    :::answer
    Entropy change from state $b$ to $c$ is

    $$
    \Delta S_{bc}=C_v\ln \frac{T_c}{T_b}
    $$

    Entropy change from state $d$ to $e$ is

    $$
    \Delta S_{de}=C_p \ln \frac{T_e}{T_d}=C_p \ln \frac{T_e}{T_b}=C_p \ln \frac{T_c}{T_b}
    $$

    Entropy change from state $e$ to $a$ is

    $$
    \Delta S_{ea}=C_v \ln \frac{T_a}{Te}=C_v \ln {T_a}{T_c}
    $$

    Since the entire process is cyclic. So,

    $$
    \Delta S_{cycle}=0 \Longrightarrow \Delta S_{bc}+\Delta S_{de}+\Delta S_{ea}=0
    $$

    Therefore, we have

    $$
    \begin{align*}
    &C_v \ln{\frac{T_c}{T_b}} + C_p \ln{\frac{T_c}{T_b}} + C_v \ln{\frac{T_a}{T_c}} = 0 \\
    &\Longrightarrow \left(C_p + C_v\right) \ln{\frac{T_c}{T_b}} + C_v \ln{\frac{T_a}{T_c}} = 0 \\
    &\Longrightarrow \left(\frac{C_p}{C_v} + 1\right) \ln{\frac{T_c}{T_b}} + \ln{\frac{T_a}{T_c}} = \ln 1 \\
    &\Longrightarrow (\gamma + 1) \ln{\frac{T_c}{T_b}} + \ln{\frac{T_a}{T_c}} = \ln 1 \\
    &\Longrightarrow \left(\frac{T_c}{T_b}\right)^{\gamma + 1} \cdot \frac{T_a}{T_c} = 1 \\
    &\Longrightarrow T_c^\gamma T_a = T_b^{\gamma + 1} \\
    &\Longrightarrow T_a = \frac{T_b^{\gamma + 1}}{T_c^\gamma}
    &\end{align*}
    $$

    Hence, option (B) is correct.
    :::

9. Air enters a pipe at 1 bar and flows isothermally at the rate of 1 kg/s. Due to pipe friction, the pressure drop between two sections of the pipe is 7% of the pressure at inlet section.    > For ideal gas undergoing isothermal process, per unit change in entropy is
   >
   > $$
   > \begin{align*}

   > &=-R\ln{\frac{p_2}{p_1}}\\

   > &=0.0208\frac{kJ}{kgK}\\

   > $$

   > $${\dot{S}}_{gen}=1\times20.8\ J/sK$$
   > Irreversibility rate
